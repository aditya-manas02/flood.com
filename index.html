Flash Floods

So what are flash floods? Flash floods are quick and lethal. Flash floods are the rapid flooding of an area depending on the intensity of rainfall and it’s duration. Flash floods can occur within minutes or hours of rainfall and strike quickly. These floods can flow for miles and at extremely fast speeds.

It is vital to your safety that you take key steps in regards to the possibility of flash flooding. You must pay attention to the warning signs and be prepared.

The Dangers!

Flash floods prove to be a danger for their force and speed. It only takes 18 inches of water to float a car off the ground and 2 feet to force it down stream. With this power in mind, flash floods can roll boulders, rip trees from the ground, and destroy buildings, roads, or bridges.The speed of flash floods is also an extreme danger. The floods can develop in minutes and take motorists and hikers by complete surprise. With only 6 inches of water being needed to sweep you off your feet, having a flash flood show up at your feet unprepared is cause for concern.

The History

Flash floods have caused notable destruction and death in the past. Even recent history has suffered from flooding. In 1993 the Great Mississippi and Missouri Rivers Flood caused 15 billion dollars in damage. 20 million acres across nine states flooded. 54,000 people had to evacuate with 50,000 homes being damaged or destroyed. Some towns were never rebuilt after complete annihilation. In March of 2019, record rain and snowfall flooded several midwest states: 3 people died, thousands evacuated, and 10.9 billion dollars of damage was done.

The Facts

Floods hold some facts you should know. Most deaths occur in cars. Around 150 people a year are killed in flooding, more than any other weather hazard. In 2018 alone, 80 people were killed by flash floods. Just one inch of water can cause $25,000 worth of damage to your home.

Before

There are statewide warnings in the instance where a flash flood has the potential to form and wreak havoc. Two types of warnings could be sent out to your cellular device if a flash flood is predicted to occur. These two types are the flood watch and flood warning. It is important to understand the differences in order for you and your family to take the proper precautions. A flood watch will be sent out when there is the potential for flooding to occur due to heavy rains forecasted. In contrast, a flood warning is when flooding is predicted to occur or has already begun to occur. Families must

pay attention to these alerts and take them very seriously, especially if they are in an area that is prone to flash floods. If there is a flood warning with a predicted flash flood, families should immediately begin to move to higher ground to avoid being caught in the worst of the flood since water always flows down to the lowest elevation. To protect their vehicles from being washed away in a flash flood; it may be a good idea to move your vehicle away from streams or other bodies of water if they are parked in close vicinity.

During

When caught in a flash flood it is important to try and remain calm so that you are able to think clearly and make smart decisions. If you are in your vehicle during a fast flood and the water is moving quickly do not exit the vehicle for risk of being swept away by the fast-moving water. Avoid driving through the fast-moving water because it is hard to gauge the true depth and just two feet of water can sweep your vehicle away. In the unfortunate event that you find yourself swept away in a flash flood, lay on your back with your head facing in the same direction as the water is flowing so you

can see what objects are coming at you from behind. While being swept away, look for anything stationary that you could safely hold onto until the flash flood is over or help arrives. If you have not been swept away by the water but are near it: you must avoid going near or trying to cross the water; it only takes six inches of fast-moving water to knock you off your feet. Always be aware of downed power lines as well since they can electrify the water surrounding them.

After

If you are an adult and are healthy enough to assist in the aftermath recovery and clean up that is highly recommended due to the vast amount of damage flash floods can cause. However, children should never assist in the clean-up efforts because as water is flooding through residential and commercial areas it is washing away a variety of different harmful chemicals that can have detrimental effects on the mental development of children.

The Beginning

Flash flooding beings with atmospheric conditions that bring on heavy rain or a large snowfall melt after a long period. The main causes of flooding are heavy and rapid rainfall, long periods of rain or snowmelt. Geographical factors that heavily contribute to flash floods are steep slopes, impermeable rock, saturated and compacted soils, or dry soil. Flash floods will typically begin suddenly but they develop overtime. If you live in areas with the aforementioned geographical traits or weather conditions, you are most likely to experience flash flooding.
The data from research around 1990 and 2006 shows that the highest number of flood cases is in November with a close second in September. With what was discussed earlier, it should not be a surprise that areas most susceptible to flash flooding have the heaviest rain in November and September.

About Us

Flash floods are rapid and extreme flow of high water into a normally dry area, or a rapid water level rise in a stream or creek above a predetermined flood level, beginning within six hours of the causative event (e.g., intense rainfall, dam failure, ice jam).

Our organization is dedicated to providing timely and accurate information on floods to ensure public safety and preparedness.

Our Mission

We aim to enhance awareness and understanding of flash flood risks through:

  • Education: Offering resources to educate the community about the dangers of flash floods and how to respond.
  • Monitoring: Utilizing advanced technology to monitor weather patterns and predict potential flash flood events.
  • Alerts: Disseminating real-time alerts to inform the public of imminent flash flood threats.
  • Research: Conducting research to improve flash flood forecasting and mitigation strategies.
